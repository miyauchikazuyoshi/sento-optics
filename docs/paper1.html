Optical Classification of Solids through Electronic Delocalization: From Diamond Transparency to Graphite Blackness via the Inverse Participation Ratio

Kazuyoshi Miyauchi (Independent researcher)

Published: 2026-03-28 · DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.19425523 · sento-optics project

PDF (English) PDF (日本語) Zenodo GitHub

Abstract

Why is graphite black while metals shine, even though both have zero band gap? The standard band-gap criterion fails for such cases. We propose that the optical response of solids is classified by two variables: an electronic delocalization index δIPR based on the inverse participation ratio (IPR) of Kohn–Sham orbitals, and an effective transport dimensionality Deff.

The product δ × (Deff + 1) classifies materials into four optical regimes: transparent (low δ), colored (intermediate δ with selective absorption), black (high δ but low Deff), and lustrous (high δ and high Deff). Tested on 5 carbon allotropes (diamond, C60, SWCNT, graphene, graphite) and h-BN: 6 of 7 materials classified correctly without any fitting parameters. Maximally localized Wannier function (MLWF) spreads provide an independent bulk measure consistent with the IPR-based classification.

The mathematical foundation is provided by the Cardenas-Castillo sum rule (2024), which proves that Wannier spread controls the frequency-integrated optical response. This work connects electronic structure to macroscopic optical appearance through a single descriptor, δ.

Significance

This paper is part of the sento-optics research program, which proposes electronic delocalization δ as a unifying descriptor for interface properties of matter. Paper 1 establishes δ for optical classification; Paper 2.1 extends it to surface tension through a two-variable (δnuc, δelec) framework.
