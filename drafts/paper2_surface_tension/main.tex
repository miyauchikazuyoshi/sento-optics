\title{Electronic Delocalization as a Descriptor for Surface Tension: \\
Connecting Inverse Participation Ratio to Miedema's Boundary Density}

\author{Kazuyoshi Miyauchi}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}

\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
Surface tension of liquid metals varies by over an order of magnitude
across the periodic table.
Density functional theory (DFT) can compute these values numerically,
and Miedema's semi-empirical model achieves $\pm 10$--$20\%$ accuracy
using the Wigner--Seitz boundary electron density~$n_\mathrm{ws}$,
but neither provides an electronic-structure descriptor
that explains \emph{why} metals with similar bulk densities
(e.g., Al and Zn, $\Delta r_s = 2.4\%$, $\Delta\gamma = 46\%$)
have vastly different surface tensions.
Here we propose that the inverse participation ratio (IPR)
of Kohn--Sham orbitals provides this missing link.
DFT calculations on metal clusters show that
the IPR-based delocalization index~$\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$
is mathematically distinct from the normalized density~$n/\bar{n}$
(mean correlation $r = 0.10$) and correlates with
boundary electron density ($r = 0.87$ for dimers).
We propose a causal chain---$\delta_\mathrm{IPR} \to n_\mathrm{ws} \to \gamma$---in
which electronic delocalization determines how much charge
reaches the cell boundary, which in turn determines surface tension
via the established Miedema framework.
This provides the first electronic-structure descriptor
connecting optical response (Paper~I) to surface tension
through a common variable:
the same~$\delta$ that classifies optical properties of carbon allotropes
also explains why Miedema's~$n_\mathrm{ws}$ predicts~$\gamma$.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% =====================================================================

Surface tension~$\gamma$ of liquid metals spans from
$\sim\!70$~mN/m (Cs) to $\sim\!1900$~mN/m (W),
governing phenomena from metal droplet formation to
wetting behavior and solidification microstructure.
Despite its technological importance, our theoretical
understanding exists at two extremes with a gap in between.

At one extreme, first-principles calculations provide
accurate numbers.
Lang and Kohn~\cite{LangKohn1970} showed that a
self-consistent jellium model gives qualitatively correct
surface energies for alkali metals,
but the model breaks down for high-density metals
($r_s < 2.5$), predicting \emph{negative} surface energies.
Stabilized jellium~\cite{Perdew1990} fixes this artifact
but remains an $r_s$-only theory, unable to distinguish metals
with similar bulk densities but different electronic structure.
Modern DFT slab calculations~\cite{Vitos1998,SinghMiller2009,Tran2016}
achieve $\pm 10\%$ accuracy across the periodic table,
but the result for each metal is a number,
not an analytical prediction from a descriptor.

At the other extreme, Miedema's semi-empirical
model~\cite{Miedema1978,deBoer1988} achieves remarkable accuracy
($\pm 10$--$20\%$) using a single parameter:
the electron density at the Wigner--Seitz cell
boundary,~$n_\mathrm{ws}$.
The formula $\gamma \propto n_\mathrm{ws}^{5/3} / V_m^{2/3}$
works across sp-metals and transition metals alike.
Yet $n_\mathrm{ws}$ is empirically calibrated---it is not
measured directly or derived from electronic structure.
The physical reason \emph{why} $n_\mathrm{ws}$ predicts
surface tension so well has not been elucidated.

The gap between these extremes is best illustrated by
the Al/Zn problem.
Aluminum ($r_s = 2.07$, $\gamma = 1140$~mN/m) and
zinc ($r_s = 2.12$, $\gamma = 782$~mN/m)
have Wigner--Seitz radii differing by only 2.4\%,
yet their surface tensions differ by 46\%.
Jellium models, which use only~$r_s$ (equivalently, bulk
density~$\bar{n}$), cannot distinguish them.
DFT can compute the correct values, but provides no
single variable that \emph{explains} the difference.
Miedema's model resolves it through
$n_\mathrm{ws}(\mathrm{Al}) > n_\mathrm{ws}(\mathrm{Zn})$,
but this merely shifts the question:
why does Al have higher boundary density than Zn?

In a companion paper~\cite{SentoOpticsPaper1}, we showed that
the optical response of carbon allotropes is classified by
a delocalization index~$\delta$ based on the inverse
participation ratio (IPR) of electronic wavefunctions,
combined with an effective transport
dimensionality~$D_\mathrm{eff}$.
Here we propose that the same~$\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$ also
explains the physical origin of Miedema's~$n_\mathrm{ws}$:
\emph{more delocalized electrons spread more uniformly across
the Wigner--Seitz cell, reaching the cell boundary in greater
density.}
This provides a causal chain
\begin{equation}
  \delta_\mathrm{IPR} \;\to\; n_\mathrm{ws} \;\to\; \gamma
  \label{eq:causal}
\end{equation}
in which the first arrow is a new connection proposed here,
and the second arrow is the established Miedema relation.

We emphasize that this work does not propose a new theory
that replaces or improves upon DFT or Miedema.
Rather, it provides a \emph{descriptor}---$\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$---that
explains \emph{why} existing theories work,
and connects surface tension to optical properties
through a common electronic-structure variable.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Jellium models and their limitations}

The jellium model replaces the discrete ionic lattice with
a uniform positive background of density
$\bar{n} = 3/(4\pi r_s^3)$.
Lang and Kohn~\cite{LangKohn1970,LangKohn1971} solved the
Kohn--Sham equations for the semi-infinite jellium surface
within the local density approximation (LDA),
obtaining surface energies that agree with experiment
for alkali metals ($r_s \gtrsim 3$) but become negative
for $r_s < 2.5$---a regime that includes Al, Zn, Cu, and
most technologically important metals.

The physical origin of this failure is that
the jellium is not mechanically stable at high densities:
the positive background exerts insufficient restoring force
to balance the electron kinetic pressure.
Perdew \textit{et al.}~\cite{Perdew1990} resolved this by
adding a structureless pseudopotential that stabilizes the
jellium at the observed equilibrium density.
This ``stabilized jellium'' gives positive surface energies
for all metals, but remains a one-parameter ($r_s$) theory
and cannot account for the electronic structure differences
between metals with similar~$r_s$.

\subsection{Miedema's boundary density}

Miedema's model~\cite{Miedema1978,deBoer1988} parameterizes
the surface energy of metals using two quantities:
the electron density at the Wigner--Seitz cell
boundary~$n_\mathrm{ws}$ and an adjusted
electronegativity~$\varphi^*$.
For elemental metals, the surface energy scales as
\begin{equation}
  \gamma \;\propto\; \frac{n_\mathrm{ws}^{5/3}}{V_m^{2/3}},
  \label{eq:miedema}
\end{equation}
where $V_m$ is the molar volume.
The physical picture is that surface energy depends on the
charge density \emph{at the cell boundary}---the region
that is ``cut'' when creating a surface.

For sp-metals, where valence electrons are nearly free,
$n_\mathrm{ws} \approx \bar{n}$.
For $d$-metals, however, $d$-electrons are spatially
localized near the nucleus and contribute less to the
boundary density: $n_\mathrm{ws} < \bar{n}$.
This distinction is what allows Miedema's model to resolve
the Al/Zn problem:
Al's sp-electrons are uniformly distributed
($n_\mathrm{ws} \approx \bar{n}$),
while Zn's filled $3d^{10}$ shell concentrates charge near
the nucleus, depleting the boundary
($n_\mathrm{ws} < \bar{n}$).

The $n_\mathrm{ws}$ values tabulated by
de~Boer \textit{et al.}~\cite{deBoer1988} are, however,
\emph{empirically calibrated} from alloy formation
enthalpies---not computed from electronic structure.
No first-principles derivation of $n_\mathrm{ws}$ exists,
and no electronic-structure descriptor has been identified
that predicts which metals will have high or low boundary
density.

\subsection{DFT slab calculations}

Modern DFT calculations using slab
geometries~\cite{Vitos1998,SinghMiller2009,Tran2016}
can compute surface energies for any metal with
$\pm 10\%$ accuracy (depending on the exchange-correlation
functional).
These calculations implicitly include all electronic-structure
effects---sp vs.\ $d$ character, band filling,
hybridization---but provide no analytical insight into
\emph{which} electronic property drives the variation of~$\gamma$.
The $d$-band center model of Hammer and
N{\o}rskov~\cite{Hammer1995}, so successful for
chemisorption energies, has not been applied to
surface energies of elemental metals.

\subsection{The inverse participation ratio}

The inverse participation ratio (IPR) quantifies the spatial
localization of a wavefunction~\cite{Bell1970,Wegner1980}.
For a state~$|\psi_n\rangle$ expanded in a local
basis~$\{|\mu\rangle\}$, the IPR is
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{IPR}_n = \sum_\mu |c_{n\mu}|^4,
  \label{eq:ipr_def}
\end{equation}
where $c_{n\mu} = \langle \mu | \psi_n \rangle$.
A perfectly delocalized state over $N$ sites has
$\mathrm{IPR} = 1/N$, while a state localized on a
single site has $\mathrm{IPR} = 1$.
The delocalization index
\begin{equation}
  \delta_\mathrm{IPR} = \frac{1}{N \cdot \langle \mathrm{IPR} \rangle}
  \label{eq:delta_ipr}
\end{equation}
ranges from $\delta = 1$ (fully delocalized) to $\delta = 1/N$
(fully localized).

In Paper~I~\cite{SentoOpticsPaper1}, we showed that
$\delta_\mathrm{IPR} \times D_\mathrm{eff}$ classifies
the optical response of carbon allotropes, from transparent
diamond ($\delta \approx 0.3$) to reflective graphite
($\delta \approx 0.8$).
Here we propose that the same quantity also determines
the Wigner--Seitz boundary density:
more delocalized electrons (higher $\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$)
spread more uniformly, delivering more charge to the
cell boundary.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{DFT cluster calculations}

To test the connection between $\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$ and
boundary density, we performed all-electron DFT calculations
on homonuclear dimers and small clusters using
PySCF~\cite{PySCF2018}.
Calculations used the B3LYP functional and def2-SVP
basis set for all elements.

For each cluster, we computed:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{IPR-based delocalization}:
    From the Kohn--Sham orbital coefficients, the Mulliken
    populations $p_\mu^{(n)} = |c_{n\mu}|^2 S_{\mu\mu}$
    were computed for each occupied orbital~$n$ on each
    basis function~$\mu$.
    The IPR per orbital is
    $\mathrm{IPR}_n = \sum_\mu (p_\mu^{(n)})^2 / (\sum_\mu p_\mu^{(n)})^2$,
    and $\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$ follows from
    Eq.~\eqref{eq:delta_ipr}.

  \item \textbf{Boundary density}:
    The electron density was evaluated at the midpoint
    between nearest-neighbor atoms---a proxy for the
    Wigner--Seitz cell boundary.
    The boundary density ratio
    $\rho_\mathrm{boundary} / \rho_\mathrm{max}$
    quantifies how much charge reaches the cell boundary
    relative to the maximum density (near the nuclei).

  \item \textbf{Normalized density}:
    For comparison, we also computed
    $\delta_\mathrm{density} = n / \bar{n}$ at the same
    grid points.
\end{enumerate}

Elements studied include Li, Na, K (alkali metals),
Be, Mg, Ca (alkaline earths), Al, Ga, Si (p-block),
and Cu, Zn (transition/post-transition metals)---11 elements
spanning a wide range of electronic structure.
For open-shell dimers (Al$_2$, Si$_2$, Ga$_2$),
unrestricted Kohn--Sham (UKS) calculations with
triplet spin state were employed.
Dimer bond lengths were taken from experimental values
(NIST Computational Chemistry Database).

\subsection{Comparison with Miedema's $n_\mathrm{ws}$}

We compared the IPR-based delocalization with the tabulated
$n_\mathrm{ws}$ values of de~Boer \textit{et al.}~\cite{deBoer1988}
for 12 metals:
Li, Na, K, Cs, Be, Mg, Al, Zn, Cu, Ga, In, Sn.
Pearson correlation, Spearman rank correlation, and
leave-one-out cross-validated $R^2$ were used as metrics.
Partial correlations controlling for $\bar{n}$ test whether
$n_\mathrm{ws}$ and $\delta$ carry information beyond
bulk density.


% =====================================================================
\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{$\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$ is distinct from $n/\bar{n}$}

The first test establishes that $\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$
computed from IPR is a genuinely different quantity from
the normalized density $\delta_\mathrm{density} = n/\bar{n}$.
Figure~\ref{fig:test1} shows the orbital-resolved comparison
for Li, Na, Be, and Mg clusters.
The mean Pearson correlation between $\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$
and $\delta_\mathrm{density}$ across all clusters is
$r = 0.10$, confirming that they are mathematically distinct.
For Na clusters, the correlation is actually
\emph{negative} ($r = -0.54$ to $-0.69$):
orbitals that are more delocalized by the IPR measure
do not necessarily correspond to higher local density.

\begin{figure}[htb]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig_test1_dft_ipr.png}
  \caption{Comparison of IPR-based $\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$ and
    density-based $\delta_\mathrm{density}$ for metal clusters
    computed with DFT (B3LYP/def2-SVP).
    Mean correlation $r = 0.10$; Na clusters show negative
    correlation, confirming these are distinct quantities.}
  \label{fig:test1}
\end{figure}

This result is critical because the original formulation
of the surface tension model (not presented here; see
supplementary tests) used $\delta = n/\bar{n}$, which
for a jellium profile trivially reduces to $\bar{n}^{1/6}$
and carries no information beyond bulk density.
$\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$, by contrast, encodes the
\emph{shape} of the wavefunction, not merely its magnitude.


\subsection{Miedema's $n_\mathrm{ws}$ dominates bulk density}

Before connecting $\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$ to $n_\mathrm{ws}$,
we verify that $n_\mathrm{ws}$ is indeed the better
predictor of~$\gamma$ compared to bulk density~$\bar{n}$.
Figure~\ref{fig:test5} shows the correlation for 12 metals.

\begin{figure}[htb]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig_test5_nws_miedema.png}
  \caption{Surface tension predictors compared across 12 metals.
    Miedema's $n_\mathrm{ws}$ ($r = 0.922$) far outperforms
    bulk electron density $\bar{n}$ ($r = 0.690$).
    The Miedema formula
    $\gamma \propto n_\mathrm{ws}^{5/3}/V_m^{2/3}$
    achieves $r = 0.936$ and LOO $R^2 = 0.778$.
    Partial correlation of $n_\mathrm{ws}$ beyond $\bar{n}$:
    $r = 0.958$.}
  \label{fig:test5}
\end{figure}

Key findings:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $n_\mathrm{ws}$ alone: $r = 0.922$ with~$\gamma$
  \item $\bar{n}$ alone: $r = 0.690$ with~$\gamma$
  \item Partial $r(n_\mathrm{ws}, \gamma \mid \bar{n}) = 0.958$:
    $n_\mathrm{ws}$ carries substantial information
    \emph{beyond} bulk density
  \item Miedema formula LOO $R^2 = 0.778$
\end{itemize}

The Al/Zn pair illustrates this clearly:
$\bar{n}$ predicts nearly identical $\gamma$
(2.4\% density difference), while
$n_\mathrm{ws}(\mathrm{Al}) / n_\mathrm{ws}(\mathrm{Zn})$
correctly predicts a large $\gamma$ ratio.


\subsection{$\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$ correlates with boundary density}

The central result of this paper connects
$\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$ to boundary density.
Figure~\ref{fig:test7} shows the DFT cluster results.

For homonuclear dimers of 11 elements spanning alkali metals,
alkaline earths, p-block metals, and transition metals,
the correlation between $\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$ and
the boundary density ratio is $r = 0.84$
($n = 11$, $p = 0.001$).
Metals with higher $\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$ (more delocalized
valence electrons) have higher electron density at the
interatomic midpoint, consistent with the proposed mechanism:
delocalized electrons spread more uniformly across the cell,
delivering more charge to the boundary.

\begin{figure}[htb]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig_test7_delta_causes_nws.png}
  \caption{DFT evidence for the $\delta_\mathrm{IPR} \to n_\mathrm{ws}$
    connection.
    Boundary electron density (at the interatomic midpoint)
    vs.\ IPR-based delocalization for metal dimers.
    Correlation $r = 0.84$ ($n = 11$, $p = 0.001$) across
    alkali, alkaline earth, p-block, and transition metals
    supports the causal chain:
    more delocalized electrons $\to$ higher boundary density
    $\to$ higher surface tension.}
  \label{fig:test7}
\end{figure}


\subsection{The Al/Zn problem explained}

The proposed framework offers a qualitative explanation
for the Al/Zn discrepancy:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Al} ($3s^2 3p^1$, pure sp):
    Valence electrons are sp-type,
    spatially extended and delocalized
    $\to$ high $\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$
    $\to$ electrons reach cell boundary
    $\to$ high $n_\mathrm{ws}$
    $\to$ high $\gamma = 1140$~mN/m.

  \item \textbf{Zn} ($3d^{10} 4s^2$):
    Although Zn has a filled $d$-shell that nominally
    does not participate in bonding, the $3d^{10}$ electrons
    are spatially concentrated near the nucleus.
    They screen the nuclear charge, causing the $4s$ electrons
    to be \emph{less} tightly bound but also \emph{less}
    uniformly distributed
    $\to$ lower $\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$ for effective boundary-reaching
    $\to$ lower $n_\mathrm{ws}$
    $\to$ lower $\gamma = 782$~mN/m.
\end{itemize}

This explanation goes beyond Miedema, who merely
\emph{tabulates} $n_\mathrm{ws}(\mathrm{Al}) > n_\mathrm{ws}(\mathrm{Zn})$,
by providing a mechanistic reason rooted in wavefunction
character.


\subsection{Spurious correlations and negative results}

Rigorous self-testing revealed several pitfalls that
constrain the claims of this work (detailed results in
supplementary materials):

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Monotonicity trap}:
    For sp-metals ordered by $r_s$, \emph{any} monotonic
    function of $r_s$ yields Spearman $\rho = 1.000$.
    The original $r = 0.956$ correlation for a jellium-based
    $\delta$-gradient integral was entirely spurious.

  \item \textbf{$\delta_\mathrm{density}$ is trivial}:
    Defining $\delta = n(z)/\bar{n}$ at a jellium surface
    gives $\int(d\delta/dz)^2\,dz \propto \bar{n}^{1/6}$---a
    trivial function of bulk density with no new physics.

  \item \textbf{Product models fail}:
    $n_\mathrm{ws} \times \delta_\mathrm{proxy}$ does not
    improve upon $n_\mathrm{ws}$ alone
    ($\Delta\mathrm{LOO}\,R^2 = +0.0006$).
    The current evidence does not support $\delta$ as an
    \emph{independent} predictor of $\gamma$.

  \item \textbf{Cluster vs.\ slab}:
    The $r = 0.84$ correlation ($n = 11$, $p = 0.001$)
    uses dimer molecules as proxies for bulk metals.
    Larger clusters ($N = 4, 6$) showed artifacts in boundary
    density evaluation due to ambiguous midpoint positions.
\end{enumerate}

These negative results are presented not as failures but as
essential constraints that sharpen the claim:
$\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$ is proposed as a \emph{descriptor}
that illuminates the physics behind $n_\mathrm{ws}$,
not as a competing predictor.


% =====================================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{What $\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$ explains that DFT does not}

DFT slab calculations~\cite{Vitos1998,Tran2016} can compute
$\gamma_\mathrm{Al} \neq \gamma_\mathrm{Zn}$, but the
output is two numbers.
The $\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$ framework translates this numerical
difference into a physical statement:
\emph{Al has higher surface tension because its sp valence
electrons are more delocalized, delivering more charge to
the cell boundary.}
This is the kind of analytical insight that enables
extrapolation, chemical intuition, and connection to other
properties---capabilities that pure numerical computation
does not provide.

\subsection{Reinterpretation of Miedema's $n_\mathrm{ws}$}

Miedema's $n_\mathrm{ws}$ is one of the most successful
empirical parameters in metallurgy, yet its physical basis
has remained obscure.
We propose that $n_\mathrm{ws}$ is a proxy for
electronic delocalization:
$n_\mathrm{ws}$ is high when electrons are delocalized
(sp-metals) and low when they are localized (d-metals),
because delocalized wavefunctions have more uniform
spatial extent, reaching the cell boundary in greater density.

This reinterpretation does not invalidate Miedema---it
explains \emph{why} $n_\mathrm{ws}$ works.
Just as the $d$-band center model~\cite{Hammer1995}
explains why chemisorption energies follow Miedema-like
trends, $\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$ explains why surface energies
follow $n_\mathrm{ws}$.

\subsection{Connection to optical response}

In Paper~I~\cite{SentoOpticsPaper1}, we showed that
$\delta_\mathrm{IPR} \times D_\mathrm{eff}$ classifies
the optical response of carbon allotropes.
The present work connects the \emph{same} $\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$
to surface tension via $n_\mathrm{ws}$.
Together, these results suggest that electronic delocalization
is a unifying variable:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Optical response}:
    $\delta \times D_\mathrm{eff} \to \varepsilon(\omega) \to R$
  \item \textbf{Surface tension}:
    $\delta \to n_\mathrm{ws} \to \gamma$
\end{itemize}

Both chains originate from the same electronic property
($\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$) but connect to different macroscopic
observables through different intermediate quantities.
No previous framework has identified a single electronic
descriptor that governs both optical and thermodynamic
interface properties.

\subsection{Unexplored territory}

A literature survey confirms that the connection
between IPR (or any delocalization index) and surface tension
is, to our knowledge, entirely unexplored:

\begin{itemize}
  \item The electron localization function
    (ELF)~has been used for bonding analysis at surfaces,
    but not for predicting~$\gamma$.
  \item The $d$-band center model~\cite{Hammer1995} predicts
    chemisorption energies, not surface energies.
  \item No published work connects participation ratio,
    Wannier function spread, or any localization measure
    to surface tension of metals.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations and future work}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item The $\delta_\mathrm{IPR} \to n_\mathrm{ws}$ correlation
    ($r = 0.84$, $n = 11$) spans diverse electronic structures
    but uses isolated dimers as proxies for bulk metals.
    Extension to periodic slab calculations is essential.

  \item The current cluster calculations use finite molecules
    as proxies for metallic surfaces.
    Self-consistent slab calculations with
    orbital-resolved IPR analysis would provide more direct
    evidence.

  \item $\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$ does not yet \emph{predict}~$\gamma$
    better than $n_\mathrm{ws}$ alone.
    The value of $\delta$ is as an \emph{explanatory}
    descriptor, not a predictive one at present.

  \item The connection to temperature dependence of surface
    tension ($d\gamma/dT$) is unexplored.
    Whether $\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$ varies with temperature
    in a way that explains surface entropy is an open question.

  \item Extension to non-metallic systems (molecular liquids,
    ionic melts) requires different definitions of
    delocalization but may follow the same conceptual framework.
\end{enumerate}


% =====================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% =====================================================================

We have proposed the inverse participation ratio of
Kohn--Sham orbitals as an electronic-structure descriptor
for surface tension of liquid metals.
Three key results support this proposal:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item $\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$ is mathematically distinct from
    the normalized density $n/\bar{n}$ (DFT verification,
    mean $r = 0.10$), establishing it as a genuinely new variable.

  \item $\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$ correlates with boundary electron
    density ($r = 0.84$, $p = 0.001$ for 11 metal dimers),
    supporting the causal chain
    $\delta \to n_\mathrm{ws} \to \gamma$.

  \item The framework explains \emph{why} Al has higher surface
    tension than Zn despite similar bulk density:
    sp-electron delocalization delivers more charge to the
    cell boundary.
\end{enumerate}

Combined with the optical classification reported in
Paper~I~\cite{SentoOpticsPaper1}, $\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$ emerges
as a candidate for a unified electronic-structure variable
connecting interface properties across optical and thermodynamic
domains.

The evidence presented here is preliminary: 11 dimer datapoints
for the central correlation, cluster models rather than
slab calculations, and no improvement in predictive accuracy
over Miedema's established model.
What $\delta_\mathrm{IPR}$ provides is not better numbers,
but a physical \emph{reason}---the first electronic-structure
descriptor proposed for surface tension across sp and $d$ metals.
